# The three laws of hard magic (adapted for this project)

These are the load-bearing principles of rules-based ("hard") magic systems in
epic fantasy, stated as working laws for this project.

## First law
The author's ability to solve conflict with magic is **directly proportional**
to how well the reader understands that magic.

If magic resolves the climax, its rules must have been clearly established
*beforehand*. Cheap deus ex machina happens when a power is invoked that the
reader did not see coming. Conversely: when the reader has watched the rules
operate for chapters, the climactic application feels inevitable in retrospect.

**Practical rule:** by the time magic resolves anything, the reader should
have seen its mechanic at least three times in lower-stakes scenes.

## Second law
**Limitations are more interesting than powers.** Costs, restrictions, and
what magic *cannot* do create drama. Powers without cost are boring.

Every significant magical action must show its price somewhere in the prose:
physical exhaustion, emotional toll, social consequence, material burn.
Reserve costless magic for true masters in moments of mastery — and then
make the next page show the long-term cost.

## Third law
**Expand what you have before adding something new.** One deep magic system
beats three shallow ones. Before introducing a new mechanic, see whether the
existing one can be re-expressed in a new way. Depth comes from variations
on the rule, not from piling on rules.

## How to apply across a book

- **Chapter 1-5:** show the magic in mundane use. Daily life. The mechanic
  visible without explanation.
- **Chapter 6-15:** stress-test the magic. Failure modes. Costs paid that
  hurt characters readers care about.
- **Chapter 16-25:** the magic becomes a tool of inevitable choice. The rules
  the reader learned earlier converge into a single problem with one answer.
- **Climax:** the magic resolves the conflict in a way the reader could have
  predicted from chapter 3. The resolution rereads as foreshadowed, even
  though the reader didn't see it coming.

## Anti-patterns specific to magic

- "Tendrils of power." Be specific about what the magic looks/sounds/smells
  like in this world, not how it looks in every fantasy book.
- "Crackling with arcane energy." If you cannot describe what the magic
  *feels like in the body*, you don't know your magic well enough yet.
- Magic that always solves problems. Magic should also create them.
- Magic that always costs the user. Sometimes it costs someone else, and
  the user knows it.
