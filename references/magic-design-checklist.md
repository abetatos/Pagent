# Magic system design checklist

Use this when filling out the **Magic system** section of `setup.md`
and when expanding it in `plan-book`. A magic system is not a list of
spells. It is a metaphor that the plot then weaponizes.

## Source
Where does the magic come from? The source determines who can access
it and who cannot, which determines social structure.

Examples:
- An external substance (Allomancy: metals).
- A relationship (Stormlight: spren).
- A physical location (Inheritance: gates).
- A bodily fact (bloodline, age, birth, sex).
- A learned skill (rigorous training, lifelong study).
- An emotional state (faith, grief, rage).

The source must be **specific**. "Energy from the world" is not a
source. "Light absorbed during storms and stored in spheres" is.

## Mechanic
How is the magic accessed, step by step? Walk through one normal use:

1. What does the user *do*?
2. What does the user *feel*?
3. What does an observer *see*?
4. How long does it take?
5. How is it stopped?

A reader should be able to *picture* one use of magic by chapter 3.

## Cost
What does using the magic cost? Pick from these (at minimum two):

- **Physical:** exhaustion, hunger, aging, scarring, blindness.
- **Emotional:** numbness, paranoia, grief amplified.
- **Material:** consumed resources that are scarce.
- **Social:** stigma, debt to an order, marked body.
- **Identity:** the user changes through use. Becomes more / less
  themselves.

**Visible in the prose.** Every meaningful use of magic shows its cost
on the page. The cost is often what makes the scene dramatic.

## Hard limitations
What can the magic **never** do? Pick at least three:

- Cannot affect a category of thing (cannot resurrect, cannot read
  minds, cannot cross water).
- Cannot persist past a duration.
- Cannot be transferred to another.
- Cannot be used twice in a row.
- Cannot be used by the wielder on themselves.

These limits are **sacred**. The drama lives in them. If a limit is
broken, the reader feels cheated.

## Who can use it
- All humans? — too broad to matter narratively.
- One class / caste / bloodline?
- People who have done something specific?
- People with a specific condition or scar?

Combine source + access rule. *"The magic comes from storms. Anyone can
access it, but only those who have survived three nights outside in a
storm without shelter can hold it steady."*

## Social consequence
How does the magic stratify society?

- Who is wealthy because of it?
- Who is afraid of it?
- Who is exploited by it?
- What rituals govern its use?
- What myths protect or condemn it?

The magic is **always** political. If your magic has no political
texture, your worldbuilding is hollow.

## Thematic role
What moral question does the magic force?

A magic of borrowed time forces: who lives longer than they should,
and who pays?

A magic of compelled honesty forces: which truths can a society
survive?

A magic of inherited debt forces: when is a child responsible for a
parent's choices?

The climax should be a use of the magic that puts the thematic
question into the protagonist's hands. They cannot escape the
question by using the magic. The magic *is* the question.

## Connection to the world

- **Geography.** Where does the magic work, where doesn't it? Why?
- **Daily life.** What does the magic make ordinary that wouldn't be?
  Light without fire? Food without farms? Travel without distance?
- **Death.** What does the magic do about death? Anything? Nothing?
  This answer shapes the whole feel of the world.
- **Children.** When does a person discover their relationship to
  the magic? Birth, puberty, ritual? This shapes coming-of-age.

## Three escalation tiers
Plan three tiers of magical capability:

1. **Common.** What most users do. Cheap. Mundane. Visible in act 1.
2. **Skilled.** What trained users can do. Costs more. Visible in act 2.
3. **Apex.** What only a few can do. Costs the user something
   permanent. Visible at the climax — and the cost is part of the
   climax.

Save tier 3 for late. If tier 3 is visible too early, the climax has
no ceiling.
