# Fantasy structural beats — a planning reference

Used by `plan-book` when designing the outline. Not a formula to follow
mechanically — a checklist of structural functions every long fantasy
needs to satisfy somewhere.

## Three-act structure (adapted for fantasy)

### Act 1 — Inhabitation (≈25% of chapters)
The reader is being *placed* in this world. The protagonist's daily
reality is grounded before stakes escalate.

Beats:
- **Hook.** A character in the world doing something that reveals
  who they are and what the world is. Not battle. Not waking up. Not
  prophecy.
- **World introduction through use.** The magic system is *seen* used
  in mundane context. The reader learns its shape without exposition.
- **Stakes laid quietly.** What this character has to lose is
  established by what they care about — a relationship, a place, an
  identity, a craft.
- **Inciting incident.** Something forces the protagonist out of the
  daily rhythm. It should feel inevitable in retrospect.
- **Refusal / displacement.** The protagonist tries to maintain the
  old life and cannot.

### Act 2A — Expansion (≈25%)
The world widens. The protagonist meets new factions, places, rules.
The magic system is shown to be more than it seemed.

Beats:
- **New world / new faction.** Geographic or social expansion.
- **Mentor & cost.** Someone teaches the protagonist something, and
  the teaching has a cost.
- **First real failure.** The protagonist tries the new rules and
  fails. The reader sees the price.
- **Subplot escalation.** Secondary storylines begin to braid in.
- **Promise of more.** A glimpse of something larger that the
  protagonist cannot yet face.

### Midpoint — Overturning (single chapter or two)
At the midpoint, **something the reader believed is overturned.**
About a character. About the magic. About who is right. The
overturning recontextualizes earlier chapters.

This is the spine of the book. Without a sharp midpoint, the second
half drifts.

### Act 2B — Convergence (≈25%)
The widened world begins to close back in. The protagonist accumulates
allies, debts, scars. Subplots converge with the main arc.

Beats:
- **Allies tested.** Relationships under strain. Loyalty challenged.
- **Magic reveals its full logic.** The third rule the reader didn't
  see yet, or the cost the reader didn't realize was always there.
- **All-is-lost.** The protagonist appears beaten by the situation,
  by their own choices, by the magic itself. The pressure is at peak.
- **Decision.** The protagonist chooses — between want and need,
  between identity and survival, between two impossible loves.

### Act 3 — Climax & resolution (≈25%)
The climax resolves *the thematic question*, not just the plot
problem. The magic, the characters, and the theme converge in a
single inevitable event.

Beats:
- **Final approach.** The protagonist moves toward the confrontation
  carrying everything they have learned and lost.
- **Climax.** The decision made at the all-is-lost moment is
  *executed*, with consequences that demonstrate the cost.
- **Resolution.** The world after. Costs are visible. Subplots close
  (or are deliberately left open with thematic resonance).
- **Last image.** The chapter ends with an image that pays back the
  hook. The book has earned its title.

## Subplot weaving

Subplots are not optional in epic fantasy. They are how the book
breathes outside the protagonist. A subplot should:

- **Have its own beginning, middle, end** — but each scaled to ~30-50%
  of the main arc.
- **Touch the main plot at three points minimum.** Two of those
  contacts should *change* the main plot.
- **Resolve before the main climax**, OR resolve *during* the climax
  in a way that enables it. Subplots that resolve after the climax
  drain energy.
- **Carry theme that the main arc cannot.** If the main plot is
  about identity, the subplot might be about loyalty — exposing a
  facet the main plot can't reach.

## Character arcs

Each major character should have a private arc separate from the
plot. The arc is described by:

- **Wound** — what hurt them before the book started, still active.
- **Want** — what they consciously pursue. Their surface goal.
- **Need** — what would actually heal them. Usually in tension with
  the want.
- **Lie they believe.** The story confronts this belief.
- **Decision.** Their arc completes when they choose between want
  and need. The choice should hurt.
- **Transformation.** Positive arc (gains need, lets go of want),
  negative arc (refuses need, keeps the lie), tragic arc (sees the
  truth too late).

## POV decisions

- **Single POV.** Higher intimacy. Reader feels close. Hard to handle
  parallel plot threads. Best for tight character-driven fantasy.
- **3-5 POVs.** Allows dramatic irony — reader holds knowledge
  characters don't. Standard for epic fantasy. Each POV should know
  things the others don't.
- **Rotating per-chapter.** Each chapter is one POV; rotation pattern
  is part of the design.
- **POV vs distance.** Close third reads almost like first; distant
  third reads like a chronicle. Pick consciously.

## Pacing rules

- **Never two action chapters back-to-back.** The reader needs to
  absorb consequences. Quiet chapters earn the loud ones.
- **Alternate emotional registers.** Confrontation → introspection.
  Revelation → world-building. Loss → texture.
- **Dwelling chapters carry weight in the second half.** The reader,
  having invested, will accept long quiet chapters at the 75% mark
  because they want to see the characters land.
